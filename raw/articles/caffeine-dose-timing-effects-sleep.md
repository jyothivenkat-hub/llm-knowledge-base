# Dose and Timing Effects of Caffeine on Sleep

## Abstract

This double-blind, placebo-controlled, randomized crossover trial tested how caffeine dose (100mg vs 400mg) and timing (4, 8, and 12 hours before bedtime) affect sleep in 23 healthy males aged 18-40. The study used polysomnography for objective sleep measurement and found dramatic dose-dependent effects.

## Key Findings

### 100mg Caffeine (One Cup of Coffee)
- Produces NO significant effect on objective or subjective sleep at any timing
- Safe to consume up to 4 hours before bedtime without measurable sleep disruption
- This is approximately one standard cup of coffee

### 400mg Caffeine (Four Cups of Coffee) — Major Sleep Disruption

#### Sleep Duration
- Total sleep time reduced by 50.6 minutes when consumed 4 hours before bed
- Still reduced by ~28.7 minutes when consumed 8 hours before bed
- Still reduced by ~30.0 minutes when consumed 12 hours before bed

#### Falling Asleep
- Sleep onset latency increased by 14.2 minutes (4-hour timing)
- Time to persistent sleep increased by 25.4 minutes (4-hour timing)

#### Sleep Fragmentation
- Wake time after sleep onset increased by 26.2 minutes (4-hour) and 29.1 minutes (8-hour)
- Awakenings per hour increased by 1.4 when consumed 4 hours before bed
- People woke up more often and stayed awake longer during the night

#### Deep Sleep Destruction
- Deep sleep (N3/slow-wave sleep) reduced by 29.7 minutes at 4 hours before bed
- Reduced by 15.3 minutes at 8 hours before bed
- Reduced by 20.6 minutes even at 12 hours before bed
- Light sleep (N1/N2) proportion increased by 5.6-5.8% across all timing conditions

### The Perception Gap — People Don't Know Caffeine Is Hurting Their Sleep
- Only 44% of participants correctly identified the caffeine dose consumed
- Only 36% identified correct timing
- Only 22% identified both correctly
- Participants reported reduced sleep quality only when 400mg was consumed 4 hours before bed
- At 8-12 hours before bed, participants felt fine despite objective measurements showing significant disruption
- This means: caffeine is silently ruining your sleep and you don't even know it

### Dose Comparison (100mg vs 400mg at Same Timing)
- 400mg reduced total sleep by 46.2 minutes MORE than 100mg at 4-hour timing
- Sleep efficiency decreased 7.1% more with 400mg at 4 hours
- Deep sleep was 33.8 minutes shorter with 400mg vs 100mg at 4-hour timing

## Practical Context
- 400mg = approximately four standard coffees, three 500mL energy drinks, or two pre-workout supplements
- 100mg = one standard cup of coffee
- Caffeine's half-life is 5-6 hours, meaning half the caffeine from a 2pm coffee is still in your system at 8pm

## Mechanism
Caffeine acts by antagonizing adenosine receptors (A1 and A2A) in the brain. Adenosine is the molecule that builds up during waking hours and creates sleep pressure. By blocking adenosine, caffeine artificially suppresses sleepiness. It also suppresses melatonin secretion and delays the biological night.

## Clinical Recommendations
- 100mg caffeine: Safe up to 4 hours before bedtime
- 400mg caffeine: Avoid within 12 hours of bedtime to prevent sleep disruption
- Key insight: You won't FEEL the disruption at 8-12 hours, but your sleep architecture is still degraded

## Methodology
- Double-blind, placebo-controlled, randomized crossover design
- 48-hour washout between conditions
- In-home polysomnography (Somfit device)
- Salivary caffeine concentration testing
- Genetic testing for caffeine metabolism variants (CYP1A2, ADORA2A)
- 7-day baseline monitoring followed by 21-day intervention

## Limitations
- Only tested young healthy males (ages 18-40)
- Single dose per condition (not cumulative daily intake)
- In-home setting without laboratory controls
