## Overview

Computational neuroscience investigates the principles underlying neural computation by developing formal models that explain how biological brains process information. Recent research has expanded this field to include [[Large Language Models]] as computational substrates for understanding [[Language Processing]] in humans, revealing unexpected structural and functional parallels between artificial and biological language systems.

## Key Findings

### Shared Computational Principles

Recent empirical work demonstrates that [[Large Language Models]] and the human brain operate according to fundamentally similar computational principles. Both systems employ **pre-onset prediction** (generating expectations before sensory input arrives) and **post-onset surprise** (updating representations when predictions are violated). This convergence suggests that [[autoregressive language models]] may instantiate core principles of biological language processing rather than merely approximating it through different mechanisms.

### Hierarchical Representation and Organization

The organizational structure of language processing appears hierarchical in both computational and biological systems. [[Layer-wise representations]] in LLMs provide interpretable proxies for neural computation, with each layer capturing progressively abstract linguistic features analogous to hierarchical processing observed in the human brain. Critically, [[attribution-based explanations]] of LLM predictions reflect the same hierarchical principles that organize biological language systems, suggesting this organizational structure is fundamental to language processing itself rather than an artifact of either implementation.

### Embedding-Based Contextual Representation

Both systems rely on [[contextual embeddings]] as a core mechanism for representing meaning. In LLMs, embeddings encode contextual information that aligns with neural representations measured via [[fMRI]]. This convergence indicates that embedding-based representation may be a universal principle of language understanding across biological and artificial systems.

## Methods

Research synthesizing computational neuroscience and LLM studies employs:

- **Layer-wise analysis**: Comparing representations across LLM layers to neural activity patterns recorded during language comprehension tasks
- **fMRI alignment studies**: Measuring correlation between LLM internal representations and human brain activation during identical language processing tasks
- **Attribution analysis**: Examining which model components contribute to predictions and comparing these explanatory structures to known hierarchical organization of language areas in the brain
- **Predictive modeling**: Testing whether computational principles (prediction, surprise) derived from neuroscience can explain LLM behavior

## Open Questions

- **Mechanistic equivalence vs. functional analogy**: Do LLMs and brains implement the same computational mechanisms, or do they achieve similar functions through fundamentally different processes?
- **Scope of alignment**: Which aspects of language processing show alignment, and which diverge? Are there language phenomena where biological and computational systems differ fundamentally?
- **Causality and learning**: Do the observed alignments reflect shared learning principles, or do they emerge from different training regimes converging on similar solutions?
- **Generalization beyond language**: Do these principles extend to other cognitive domains (reasoning, planning, perception) or are they specific to language processing?
