## Overview

Melatonin is a hormone produced by the pineal gland that regulates the [[circadian rhythm]] and promotes sleep onset. Recent research has identified melatonin suppression as a critical mechanism through which modern technology, caffeine, and stress disrupt sleep quality—particularly in adolescents and young adults. The production and timing of melatonin is sensitive to specific light wavelengths, pharmacological agents, and psychological factors, making it a convergence point for multiple sleep-disrupting pathways.

## Key Findings

### Blue Light Suppression Mechanism

Blue light wavelengths between 460-480 nm produce the most substantial melatonin suppression. This wavelength range corresponds precisely to the light emitted by smartphones, laptops, and tablets—devices ubiquitously used by students during evening hours. Quantitatively, 2 hours of LED tablet exposure causes a 55% decrease in melatonin levels and delays melatonin onset by 1.5 hours compared to reading printed material under low light. This creates a direct mechanistic link between screen use and [[circadian rhythm]] disruption.

### Caffeine-Mediated Suppression

Melatonin suppression represents one of two distinct mechanisms through which [[caffeine]] disrupts sleep. Beyond blocking adenosine receptors (A1 and A2A) to reduce sleep pressure accumulation, caffeine actively suppresses melatonin secretion, thereby delaying the biological night. This dual-mechanism action suggests that caffeine's sleep-disrupting effects operate through both homeostatic and circadian pathways simultaneously.

### Technology-Stress-Sleep Pathway

[[Smartphone dependence]] mediates stress-induced sleep disruption through dual mechanisms: psychological dependence maintains arousal while blue light suppresses melatonin production. This dual-pathway model reveals that melatonin suppression is not merely a direct photobiological effect but also functions as part of a broader technology-mediated stress response. The convergence of psychological and physiological mechanisms suggests that interventions targeting only one pathway may have limited efficacy.

## Methods

Melatonin suppression has been measured through:
- **Circulating melatonin levels** via blood sampling or saliva collection following controlled light exposure
- **Melatonin onset timing** compared across conditions (e.g., LED tablet use vs. printed material reading)
- **Quantitative assessment** of suppression magnitude (e.g., percentage decrease from baseline)
- **Longitudinal mediation analysis** to assess melatonin's role in stress-sleep pathways

## Open Questions

- **Genetic vulnerability**: Individual susceptibility to melatonin suppression varies significantly based on genetic polymorphisms in circadian genes (particularly *PER3*, *RORB*, and *CRY1*). How do these genetic factors interact with blue light exposure and caffeine sensitivity?
- **Dose-response relationships**: What is the minimum blue light exposure duration and intensity required to produce clinically significant melatonin suppression?
- **Interaction effects**: How do caffeine and blue light exposure interact when combined (e.g., evening caffeine consumption while using screens)?
- **Recovery kinetics**: How quickly does melatonin production normalize after blue light exposure ceases?
- **Intervention optimization**: Should interventions targeting melatonin suppression be personalized based on genetic background and technology use patterns?
