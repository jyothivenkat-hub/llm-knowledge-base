## Overview

Neural architecture refers to the organizational structure of information processing systems, encompassing both biological brains and artificial neural networks. Recent research reveals striking parallels between the hierarchical and distributed design principles underlying [[Large Language Models]] and human [[Language Processing|language processing systems]], suggesting convergent evolution toward similar computational solutions for language comprehension.

## Key Findings

### Distributed Processing Rather Than Strict Hierarchy

Contrary to traditional feedforward models, both LLMs and human brains employ a **"soft hierarchy"** in language processing. While higher-order regions in the brain prioritize semantic information and transformer layers capture increasingly abstract representations, lower-order areas and early layers simultaneously process acoustic/surface features and decode meaning. This bidirectional information flow contradicts rigid hierarchical assumptions and suggests that language understanding emerges from parallel, overlapping processing streams rather than sequential stages.

### Hemispheric and Network Distribution

LLM processing engages computational principles analogous to human [[Hemispheric Lateralization|hemispheric organization]], recruiting both left-hemispheric language-specific systems and right-hemispheric networks supporting higher-order cognitive demands. This distributed architecture enables the complex, context-dependent reasoning required for language comprehension—a principle that appears fundamental to both biological and artificial systems.

### Convergent Architectural Principles

The structural alignment between [[Transformer Architecture|transformer layer organization]] and brain processing hierarchies suggests that LLMs may have converged on organizational principles evolved in human neural language systems. This convergence is not coincidental but reflects underlying computational constraints and efficiency principles that favor certain architectural solutions for language tasks.

### Residual Connections and Cortical Circuits

[[Residual Connections|Residual connections]] in transformers map to effective connectivity patterns within cortical circuits, providing a computational explanation for skip connections observed in neural architecture. These connections enable information to bypass intermediate processing stages, mirroring the brain's use of parallel pathways and recurrent feedback loops that enhance both efficiency and robustness.

## Methods

Research synthesizing neural architecture across biological and artificial systems employs:
- **Layer-wise embedding analysis** comparing transformer layer representations to fMRI data from human language processing
- **Hierarchical decomposition** of both brain regions and model layers to identify processing stages
- **Connectivity mapping** between transformer residual connections and measured cortical effective connectivity
- **Comparative neuroscience** examining organizational principles across species and systems

## Open Questions

- **Causal mechanisms**: Do transformer architectures genuinely implement brain-like principles, or do surface similarities mask fundamentally different computational processes?
- **Functional equivalence**: Which aspects of neural architecture are necessary for language understanding, and which are implementation details?
- **Evolutionary origins**: Did human language systems evolve toward transformer-like principles, or do both systems independently discover optimal solutions?
- **Temporal dynamics**: How do the temporal properties of biological neural processing (millisecond-scale) relate to transformer inference (token-by-token)?
- **Embodiment and grounding**: How do abstract architectural principles account for the role of embodied experience in human language understanding?
