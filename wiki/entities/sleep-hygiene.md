## Overview

Sleep hygiene encompasses behavioral and environmental practices that promote consistent, restorative sleep. Recent research reveals that effective sleep hygiene interventions operate across multiple pathways—circadian regulation, pharmacological timing, and psychological stress management—with varying degrees of efficacy depending on the specific behavior and individual circumstances.

## Key Findings

### Circadian and Light-Based Interventions

[[Blue light exposure]] from electronic devices represents a significant sleep disruptor, particularly in adolescents and young adults. While blue light blocking glasses provide measurable benefit, they function as a partial mitigation rather than a complete solution. The most effective intervention remains **reducing screen time by 2+ hours before bedtime**, suggesting that the timing and duration of exposure matters more than technological compensation alone.

### Pharmacological Timing: Caffeine

Caffeine's impact on sleep is highly dose and timing-dependent. Clinical evidence establishes that **100mg consumed up to 4 hours before bedtime produces no measurable sleep disruption**, while **400mg should be avoided within 12 hours of bedtime** due to persistent degradation of sleep architecture. This wide safety window for lower doses suggests that moderate caffeine consumption can be compatible with good sleep hygiene when properly timed, though higher doses require substantially longer washout periods.

### Psychological Pathways: Stress and Smartphone Dependence

[[Smartphone dependence]] functions as a critical intervention point in the stress-sleep relationship, operating through both direct mechanisms (stimulation, [[circadian rhythm]] disruption) and indirect pathways (amplifying rumination and [[social anxiety]]). This positions smartphone use reduction not merely as a screen hygiene issue, but as a stress-management intervention with downstream sleep benefits.

## Methods

Sleep hygiene has been studied through:
- **Experimental designs**: Randomized clinical crossover trials measuring caffeine dose-response effects on sleep architecture
- **Longitudinal mediation analysis**: Examining causal pathways between stress, psychological factors, and sleep outcomes
- **Intervention studies**: Comparing technological solutions (blue light glasses) against behavioral modifications (screen time reduction)

## Open Questions

- **Dose-response curves**: While 100mg and 400mg caffeine thresholds are established, the precise dose-response relationship between 100-400mg remains unclear
- **Individual variation**: How do age, baseline caffeine sensitivity, and circadian chronotype modify the effectiveness of these interventions?
- **Interaction effects**: How do multiple sleep hygiene factors (e.g., caffeine + screen time + stress) interact when combined?
- **Long-term adherence**: Do behavioral interventions (screen reduction) maintain effectiveness over extended periods, or does adaptation occur?
- **Smartphone displacement**: If smartphone use is reduced, what replacement activities optimize sleep outcomes?
